\chapter{Results}\label{chap:results}
This chapter presents the results of running METIS with NVOL. Throughout this chapter we will compare these results to METIS own total communication volume objective. To generate the graphs and plots in this chapter the tools described in Chapter~\ref{chap:NVOL tooling} were used. These results show the impact of applying a partition-aware objective function and compare whether this leads to any improvements in the resulting partitioning. 

\section{Graphs used for testing}
To evaluate NVOL we used symmetric matrices drawn from two datasets, treating each matrix as the adjacency representation of an undirected graph. Symmetry is required because the GPP, as defined in Section~\ref{sec:gpp}, is formulated on undirected graphs. The two datasets were chosen to cover a wide range of graph
sizes and to include instances arising from different application domains. We tried to prioritize graphs with a higher average degree as well. 

The first dataset is taken from \textit{Repository for modelling acute myocardial ischemia: simulation scripts and torso-heart mesh}~\cite{martinez-navarro2019a}, who simulate acute myocardial ischemia in a human heart using
the Chaste finite-element framework. The resulting meshes were then translated into a format accepted by METIS. Table~\ref{tab:heart_dataset} summarises the seven instances, ranging from roughly $10^3$ to almost $10^7$ vertices.

\section{Maximum outgoing volume per-partition}
Using the graphs listed above, we ran METIS with two objectives: NVOL and the standard total communication volume. For each run we then used
\texttt{calc\_partitions} to analyze the resulting partitions and compare their outgoing volumes. The number of partitions used for each graph depended on the
graph's size. To avoid splitting a graph into too many pieces, we required the square of the partition count to be at most equal to the number of vertices in the graph.

The following sections will go through the results from each dataset, specifically comparing the maximum outgoing partition volume between METIS and NVOL. Using METIS as the baseline, we report each value as the percentage difference of NVOL relative to METIS, (METIS - NVOL) / METIS $\times 100$. Cells are color-coded for readability: blue indicates that NVOL achieves a lower maximum (better), while orange indicates a higher maximum (worse). Shading intensity reflects the magnitude of the difference, with darker shades marking larger improvements or regressions. Some cells are marked with N/A, which simply means that the graph was not run with that number of partitions.

Table~\ref{tab:heart_NVOLvsMETIS} shows the results for the Heart dataset. The first thing to notice is that NVOL improves the maximum outgoing communication volume in almost all runs. On average NVOL achieves a 4.7\% lower maximum outgoing volume than METIS, with the improvement laying mostly between 2\% and 7\% and peaking at 17.57\%. As was already apparent from Table~\ref{tab:heart_dataset}, these graphs are very similar in structure: they have comparable standard deviations and a very close average number of edges per vertex. This similarity is also reflected in the partitioning results, as most values within each column are relatively close to one another, with only a few exceptions.

It is interesting to note that NVOL generally seems to improve as the number of partitions increases. On average, NVOL performs two to three times as many moves as METIS during a single refinement round, and the number of partitions clearly amplifies this effect. This allows NVOL to explore more options and increases the chance of finding a better partition. Additionally, since more partitions lead to more moves, they also reduce the likelihood of getting stuck in a local minimum.

NVOL fails on 4 occasions: heart01 with $k=2$, heart04 with $k=2$, heart05 with $k=512$, and heart07 with $k=2$. Almost all of the failures occur in the runs with $k=2$. A decisive factor is that having only two partitions severely limits the possible moves. Another likely cause is that, at each refinement step, NVOL sets a ceiling on the outgoing volume of a partition and then allows any move in which the affected partitions do not exceed this maximum. When the number of partitions is large, it is easier to find a move that decreases this value, however, with only two partitions, it is more likely that no available move can improve it. NVOL therefore tends to converge on a local minimum, which may result in a higher maximum outgoing volume than METIS.

Interestingly, Figure~\ref{fig:heart_stddev2} reveals that in every 2-partition run, the standard deviation for NVOL is 0. This means that both partitions have exactly the same outgoing volume, in contrast to METIS, whose standard deviation grows progressively worse as the graph size increases. By only allowing moves that maintain or decrease the maximum outgoing volume, NVOL drives the partitions closer together.

NVOL achieves a slightly lower standard deviation than METIS in almost all runs, indicating a more even distribution of outgoing volume across partitions. All the partitioned graphs in the heart dataset seem to follow the same pattern where the standard deviation peaks at $k=8$ and then gradually decreases as the number of partitions grows, regardless of objective function. NVOL maintains an edge throughout, generally having the best difference for $k=2$ and $k=4$ partitions. Table~\ref{tab:heart4_comparison} presents the standard deviations for METIS and NVOL on heart04, which sits in the middle of the dataset and is representative of this behavior; the other graphs show similar numbers.

Both objective functions produce similar distributions of values, but NVOL consistently yields lower standard deviation. This is because NVOL favors moves that bring partition volumes closer together, whereas METIS's purely greedy approach optimizes only for volume reduction. As a result, NVOL's partitions are more evenly balanced.

Table~\ref{tab:heart4_comparison} also reveals that NVOL produces a lower total communication volume in almost every case, not just for heart04, but across the entire heart dataset. On heart04, NVOL loses twice ($k=2$ and $k=8$). When it wins, the improvement is small, but notable: NVOL beats METIS on the very metric METIS optimizes for when run with the volume objective. Given the regular structure of the heart graphs, it appears that the better-balanced partitions NVOL produces tend to indirectly reduce total communication volume as well. By reducing spikes of outgoing volume in some partitions, NVOL appears to reduce the total volume as well.

Aside from the 2-partition cases discussed earlier, heart05 with $k=512$ is the only graph–partition combination in the heart dataset where NVOL produces a higher maximum outgoing volume than METIS. The exact cause is hard to pin down from the data alone; NVOL probably made some moves from which no further improvement could be found. However, with some simple tuning (Section~\ref{sec:tuning}), NVOL recovers a win on this case, suggesting it had indeed settled into a local minimum it could not escape. Even in this failure, the loss is narrow: NVOL exceeds METIS's maximum outgoing volume by only 1.16\%, while still producing 1\% lower total communication volume and a lower standard deviation than METIS.
