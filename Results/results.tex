\chapter{Results}\label{chap:results}
This chapter presents the results of running METIS with NVOL. Throughout this chapter we will compare these results to METIS own total communication volume objective. To generate the graphs and plots in this chapter the tools described in Chapter~\ref{chap:NVOL tooling} were used. These results show the impact of applying a partition-aware objective function and compare whether this leads to any improvements in the resulting partitioning. 

\section{Graphs used for testing}
To evaluate NVOL we used symmetric matrices drawn from two datasets, treating each matrix as the adjacency representation of an undirected graph. Symmetry is required because the GPP, as defined in Section~\ref{sec:gpp}, is formulated on undirected graphs. The two datasets were chosen to cover a wide range of graph
sizes and to include instances arising from different application domains. We tried to prioritize graphs with a higher average degree as well. 

The first dataset is taken from \textit{Repository for modelling acute myocardial ischemia: simulation scripts and torso-heart mesh}~\cite{martinez-navarro2019a}, who simulate acute myocardial ischemia in a human heart using
the Chaste finite-element framework. The resulting meshes were then translated into a format accepted by METIS. Table~\ref{tab:heart_dataset} summarises the seven instances, ranging from roughly $10^3$ to almost $10^7$ vertices.

\section{Maximum outgoing volume per-partition}
Using the graphs listed above, we ran METIS with two objectives: NVOL and the standard total communication volume. For each run we then used \texttt{calc\_partitions} to analyze the resulting partitions and compare their outgoing volumes. The number of partitions used for each graph depended on the graph's size. To avoid splitting a graph into too many pieces, we required the square of the partition count to be at most equal to the number of vertices in the graph.

It is interesting to mention that for a graph run with the same parameters, METIS will produce the exact same output. This might seem confusing at first given the non-deterministic nature of the algorithm used for the initial partition described in Section~\ref{sec:initial_partitioning}. METIS uses a fixed default seed, so the "random" choice is the same across re-runs. In other words, if the seed is not manually passed, the initial partition will always be the same when running a graph with same parameters multiple times, which translates to the final output as well.

The following sections will go through the results from each dataset, specifically comparing the maximum outgoing partition volume between METIS and NVOL. Using METIS as the baseline, we report each value as the percentage difference of NVOL relative to METIS, (METIS - NVOL) / METIS $\times 100$. Cells are color-coded for readability: blue indicates that NVOL achieves a lower maximum (better), while orange indicates a higher maximum (worse). Shading intensity reflects the magnitude of the difference, with darker shades marking larger improvements or regressions. Some cells are marked with N/A, which simply means that the graph was not run with that number of partitions.

It is interesting to note that NVOL generally seems to improve as the number of partitions increases. On average, NVOL performs two to three times as many moves as METIS during a single refinement round, and the number of partitions clearly amplifies this effect. This allows NVOL to explore more options and increases the chance of finding a better partition. Additionally, since more partitions lead to more moves, they also reduce the likelihood of getting stuck in a local minimum. This does, however, greatly affect the time NVOL takes to partition which averages between 7 and 11 times slower than METIS across the heart dataset.

The runs where NVOL loses are: heart01 with $k=2$, heart04 with $k=2$, heart05 with $k=512$, and heart07 with $k=2$. Almost all of the failures occur when $k=2$. A decisive factor is that having only two partitions severely limits the possible moves. Another likely cause is that, at each refinement step, NVOL sets a ceiling on the outgoing volume of a partition and then allows any move in which the affected partitions do not exceed this maximum. When the number of partitions is large, it is easier to find a move that decreases this value, however, with only two partitions, it is more likely that no available move can improve it. NVOL therefore tends to converge on a local minimum, which may result in a higher maximum outgoing volume than METIS.

Interestingly, Figure~\ref{fig:heart_stddev2} reveals that in every 2-partition run, the standard deviation for NVOL is 0. This means that both partitions have exactly the same outgoing volume, in contrast to METIS, whose standard deviation grows progressively worse as the graph size increases. By only allowing moves that maintain or decrease the maximum outgoing volume, NVOL drives the partitions closer together.

Both objective functions produce similar distributions of values, but NVOL consistently yields lower standard deviation. This is because NVOL favors moves that bring partition volumes closer together, whereas METIS's purely greedy approach optimizes only for volume reduction. As a result, NVOL's partition volumes are more evenly balanced.

Table~\ref{tab:heart4_comparison} also reveals that NVOL produces a lower total communication volume in many runs, not just for heart04, but across the entire heart dataset. On heart04, NVOL loses twice ($k=2$ and $k=8$). When it wins, the improvement is small, but notable: NVOL beats METIS on the very metric METIS optimizes for when run with the volume objective. Given the regular structure of the heart graphs, it appears that the better-balanced partitions NVOL produces tend to indirectly reduce total communication volume as well. 

This increased balance does come with some trade offs. Figure~\ref{fig:heart4_p4dist} shows a plot of the outgoing volumes of heart04 when partitioned with $k=4$. The blue bars represent METIS and the orange ones NVOL. By looking closely at each bar one might notice that NVOL's bars are all higher than METIS's apart from the tallest one. This makes sense as NVOL moves the vertices in a way that pushes down the maximum potentially at the cost of other partitions. As a result the pattern shown in Figure~\ref{fig:heart4_p4dist} can be found in most results from NVOL.

Aside from the 2-partition cases discussed earlier, heart05 with $k=512$ is the only graph–partition combination in the heart dataset where NVOL produces a higher maximum outgoing volume than METIS. The exact cause is hard to pin down from the data alone; NVOL probably made some moves from which no further improvement could be found. However, with some simple tuning (Section~\ref{sec:tuning}), NVOL recovers a win on this case, suggesting it had indeed settled into a local minimum it could not escape. Even in this failure, the loss is narrow: NVOL exceeds METIS's maximum outgoing volume by only 1.16\%. It does, however, produce 1\% lower total communication volume and a lower standard deviation than METIS.

Throughout the Heart dataset, NVOL improves the maximum outgoing communication volume in 57 out of 61 runs (4.7\% on average, peaking at 17.57\%) and produces more evenly balanced partition volumes in almost every run. It also yields slightly lower total communication volume in many cases, despite not optimizing for it directly. The main weakness is at $k=2$, where the constrained move space limits NVOL's ability to lower the maximum outgoing volume. NVOL does, however, produce perfectly even partitions in these cases.

\subsubsection{italy\_osm}
NVOL struggles greatly with the italy\_osm graph, achieving its second worst result across all our runs of -21.21\% at $k=512$ and failing to improve on METIS's standard deviation in most runs. The italy\_osm graph represents the Italian road network extracted from OpenStreetMap, with vertices placed at intersections and edges corresponding to the road segments between them. Road networks differ structurally from the regular meshes of the heart dataset in several important ways. They are very sparse, with vertex degrees rarely exceeding 5. More importantly, they are strongly heterogeneous: dense urban clusters are connected by long, thin stretches of rural roads, and natural features such as rivers, mountains and coastlines act as small separators between regions of very different sizes.

This heterogeneity makes it noticeably harder for NVOL to push down the maximum outgoing volume while keeping partitions balanced. A partition containing a dense urban area cannot easily be matched in volume to one covering a sparse rural region, so the moves NVOL relies on to equalize partition volumes have limited room to operate. The low average degree compounds the issue, since each move affects only a handful of edges and therefore shifts the outgoing volume by very little, leaving NVOL with little leverage to escape configurations where the maximum cannot be lowered.

Despite the negative overall picture, NVOL is not uniformly worse on this graph: wins and losses occur in roughly equal numbers, with a best result of 8.87\% at $k=32$. The losses are simply larger in magnitude than the wins, which drags the average into negative territory. This suggests NVOL can occasionally find a favorable configuration even on irregular structures, but the sparse and heterogeneous nature of road networks does not seem to benefit from its partition-aware optimization, if anything, it works against it.

\subsubsection{coPapersDBLP}
The graph yielding the best and worst result is coPapersDBLP in table~\ref{tab:suitesparse}. coPapersDBLP is a citation network derived from the DBLP computer science bibliography. Citation networks have a different structure from the meshes and road networks discussed earlier. They are scale-free, with a heavy-tailed degree distribution: a small number of hub vertices carry a disproportionate share of the connections, while a long tail of vertices have only a handful. This is reflected in coPapersDBLP itself, where the maximum degree of 3299 sits orders of magnitude above the average degree of 56. Citation networks are also small-world, with short average path lengths despite the network's size, and exhibit strong community structure corresponding to research areas or fields. This means that the average shortest distance between any two vertices grows very slowly with the graph size. 

The results seem to follow a trend where they improve as the number of partitions increases. Despite the 3 negative results, the positive ones outweigh them, giving the graph an average difference of 12.56\%, the third highest in table~\ref{tab:suitesparse_METISvsNVOL_avgs}. NVOL produces much more balanced results here as well. Table~\ref{tab:suitesparse_2pComparison} already showed that $k=2$ NVOL achieves a standard deviation of 5.5 against METIS's 8,854. 

These large differences persist for $4\leq k \leq 32$. Figure~\ref{fig:coPapers_stddev} shows the standard deviations across all coPapersDBLP runs. Comparing this with the percentage differences in table~\ref{tab:suitesparse_METISvsNVOL} suggests a plausible explanation. The heavy-tailed degree distribution of citation networks makes it difficult to produce a balanced, low-volume result when partitions are few. A few handful hub vertices end up dominating their host partition's outgoing volume, and without many options for moves NVOL is unable to redistribute this volume efficiently. In fact, as the number of partitions increase, it is easier to find optimal moves for such heavy hub vertices, in turn making the heavy tailed structure easier to exploit. 
 
\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{pictures/coPapers_stddev}
	\caption{Standard deviation of partition volumes for all coPapersDBLP runs}
	\label{fig:coPapers_stddev}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:coPapers_p8dist} shows an example that supports this hypothesis. At $k=8$, NVOL achieves an almost perfectly balanced distribution with a standard deviation of 0.33 against METIS's 10,473. As with the heart04 case discussed earlier, this balance comes at the cost of pushing each partition's volume closer to each other. NVOL does not manage to improve on the maximum volume, losing by 8.82\% to METIS, and as it is visible in the figure, all the partitions are pushed closer to each other.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{pictures/coPapers_p8dist}
	\caption{Distribution of partition volumes in coPapersDBLP $k=8$}
	\label{fig:coPapers_p8dist}
\end{figure}

At high partition counts, NVOL's advantage on coPapersDBLP becomes substantial: the maximum outgoing volume improves by 40.37\%, 48.44\%, and 56.70\% over METIS at $k=128$, $k=256$, and $k=512$ respectively. Figure~\ref{fig:coPapers_p128dist} illustrates the $k=128$ case, NVOL's partition volumes cluster tightly around the mean while still achieving a significantly lower maximum than METIS. The struggle at low $k$ and these gains at high $k$ are two sides of the same coin: the heavy-tailed degree distribution that prevents balancing with few partitions becomes a feature NVOL can exploit once $k$ is large enough to spread the hubs across many partitions. Citation networks therefore appear to be a graph class where NVOL's balancing strategy has real potential, provided $k$ is sufficiently high.
