\chapter{Communication Volume Calculation} \label{cv_calculation}
This chapter will show how the change in communication volume is calculated. The chapter will begin by explaining how the total communication volume objective works, followed by a definition of how our new partition-aware objective, NVOL, works. This chapter will only focus on how gain is evaluated and implemented in the multilevel framework for communication volume. The next chapter will show how a vertex move is chosen using the results from this calculation. 

The total communication volume objective seeks to improve the total communication volume of a partitioned graph. Even though this metric is more representative of actual communication than edge cut, it does not necessarily produce a well balanced result. The net change in total communication volume after a potential move might hide bottlenecks and overloaded partitions. In contrast, NVOL delivers specific information about how the volume of each individual partition is affected by a move. Since NVOL was implemented on top of the METIS framework we will first spend some time formalizing how METIS and other partitioners use total communication volume as an objective in Section~\ref{METIS gain calculation}. In Section~\ref{Partition specific gain calculation} we present the logic behind NVOL, and how we are able to evaluate how partitions are affected by a potential move.

\section{Basic definitions}
To facilitate understanding of the implementation (see Section~\ref{cv_calc_implementation}), we begin by introducing a formal definition of communication volume. Let $G = (V, E)$ be a graph, and let $P$ be the partition vector, where $P[v]$ is the partition a vertex \textit{v} is assigned to. A vertex $u$ is defined as a neighbor of $v$ if there exists an edge $\{u,v\} \in E$. Any vertex adjacent to at least one vertex belonging to a different partition is said to lie on the boundary. We denote the set of all boundary vertices by $V_b \subseteq V$. Formally, a vertex $v \in V_b$ if there exists at least one neighbor $u \in V$ such that $\{v,u\} \in E$ and $P[v] \neq P[u]$.

For each boundary vertex $v \in V_b$, let $N_v$ denote the set of distinct partitions (excluding $P[v]$) hosting its neighboring vertices. The number of partitions contained in $N_v$, defined as $\lvert N_v \rvert$, is the outgoing communication volume of vertex $v$. Note that the number of edges incident to $v$ is not directly relevant for this measure, only the number of distinct neighboring partitions. In Figure~\ref{1}, vertex $A$ has a communication volume of 2, as it is connected to vertices in two distinct partitions. In contrast, Figure~\ref{2} illustrates a case where vertex $A$ has two edges, both connecting to vertices within the same partition. Consequently, the communication volume of vertex $A$ is 1, since only a single distinct external partition is involved.

The total communication volume of the partitioned graph is obtained by summing the outgoing volumes over all boundary vertices:
\begin{equation} \label{total_volume}
	\mathrm{total\quad volume} = \sum_{v \in V_b} \left| N_v \right|w(v)
\end{equation}
Here, $w(v)$ denotes the weight associated with vertex $v$, which scales its contribution to the outgoing communication volume. Given the structure of the multilevel approach, regardless of a unit vertex weight graph, the vertices might have weights during uncoarsening (see Section~\ref{sec:uncoarsening}). In Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example}, all vertices are assumed to have a unit weight. The boundary set is $V_b = \{A, B, C, D, E\}$. Note that vertex \textit{F} is not part of this set, as it has no edges connecting to vertices in other partitions.

Applying Equation~\ref{total_volume}, the total communication volume is computed by summing the contributions of all vertices in the boundary set. Vertices \textit{A}, \textit{C}, and \textit{D} each contribute a volume of 2, while vertices \textit{B} and \textit{E} each contribute a volume of 1. This results in a total communication volume of 8.

\section{Total gain calculation} \label{METIS gain calculation}
To determine which vertices should be moved, and to which partitions, the gain need to be evaluated for any potential move. The gain represents the change in total communication volume resulting from relocating a vertex to a new partition. A positive gain indicates a reduction in total communication volume, whereas a negative gain corresponds to an increase.
%%Should need some rephrasing about how this is deduced from METIS and how I could not find it anywhere.

Since communication only arises when an edge connects two vertices belonging to different partitions, a vertex move can only affect the communication volume of its neighboring partitions. For each boundary vertex $i \in V_b$, a partitioner needs to examine all its adjacent vertices $v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$, where $V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i \subseteq V$ denotes the set of vertices adjacent to $i$. When considering moving a boundary vertex \textit{i}, the gain calculation depends on the relationship that \textit{i} and each of its neighboring vertices have with other partitions. The effect on volume depends on which partitions they share. In particular, we define for every vertex \textit{i} and every neighbor vertex \textit{v}
\begin{equation}
	N_{i-v} = N_i \setminus (N_v \cup P[v])
	\quad \text{and} \quad
	N_{iv} = (N_i \cap N_v) \cup P[v]
\end{equation}
representing partitions exclusive to $i$ and partitions shared by $i$ and $v$, respectively. In both cases, we add in $P[v]$ as this partition is shared even though it is not part of the $N_v$ set. A vertex \textit{v} has access to its own partition $P[v]$, meaning that $P[v]$ needs to be removed in $N_{i-v}$ and added in $N_{iv}$ to precisely determine shared partitions. Notice how both sets are subsets of $N_i = N_{i-v} \cup N_{iv}$.

As a concrete example, consider Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example} and suppose the candidate vertex is~$A$, with neighbor~$B$ under consideration. Here, $N_A = \{\mathrm{blue}, \mathrm{green}\}$, while $N_B = \{\mathrm{blue}\}$. Consequently, $N_{A-B} = \{\text{green}\}$, since green is the only partition connected to~$A$ but not to~$B$, whereas $N_{AB} = \{\mathrm{blue}\}$ as both vertices have at least one edge to the blue partition. Keeping \textit{A} as the candidate vertex, consider now neighbor~$C$ with $N_C = \{\mathrm{red}, \mathrm{green}\}$. \textit{A} and \textit{C} have access to the same partitions, meaning that $N_{A-C} = \emptyset$ and $N_{AC} = \{\mathrm{blue}, \mathrm{green}\}$. This example highlights the need for adding $P[C]$ to the sets. The sets would have been $N_{A-C} = \{\mathrm{blue}\}$, $N_{AC} = \{\mathrm{green}\}$ if we had not accounted for $P[C]$. However, by simply viewing Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example} we can see that the blue partition is shared and not unique to \textit{A}.

Let $C_i[n]$ denote the number of edges connecting $i$ to partition $n$. In Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example}, for example, $C_A[\mathrm{blue}] = 1$, as \textit{A} has only one edge connected to the blue partition, while $C_D[\mathrm{blue}] = 2$, as \textit{D} has two edges connected to the blue partition. Let $G_i$ be a vector with a number of elements equal to the number of partitions, initialized at 0, where $G_i[n]$ represents the total gain obtained by moving vertex $i$ to partition $n$. For a candidate vertex \textit{i} being considered for a move, the gain contributions to all possible destination partitions are evaluated for each neighbor $v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$ as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\phantom{1.}]\label{METIS CASES}
	\item \textbf{Case 1.} \hypertarget{METIS_case_1} If $P[v] = P[i]$, then moving $i$ to any partition in $k \in N_{i-v}$ creates a shared inter-partition connection for $v$, yielding
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 2.}\hypertarget{METIS_case_2} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] = 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_{iv}$ eliminates an inter-partition connection for $v$, yielding 
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 3.}\hypertarget{METIS_case_3} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] > 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_{i-v}$ introduces a new inter-partition connection for $v$, resulting in a gain contribution of 
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 4.}\hypertarget{METIS_case_4} If $C_i[P[i]] = 0$, i.e., $i$ has no internal degree, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_i$ yields an additional gain contribution of
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
For every adjacent boundary vertex $v$ visited, we check each of the cases above for every partition in $N_{iv}$, $N_{i-v}$ or $N_i$, depending on the case. These cases represent all possible changes in communication volume when moving a vertex. Algorithm~\ref{alg:cap} in Section~\ref{cv_calc_implementation} shows how the gain is calculated for every vertex $i$ being considered moved. In turn, Section~\ref{sec:METIS refinement} shows how the gain vector $G$ is used during refinement to decide vertex moves.

\section{Partition specific gain calculation} \label{Partition specific gain calculation}
This section extends the total communication volume gain calculation from Section~\ref{METIS gain calculation}. The total gain contains no information about which partition is affected by a move, information that is required to use NVOL during refinement. Here we show how to decompose the communication volume gain into its individual contributions to each partition. Once computed, this decomposition can be used to refine the graph; how NVOL uses it is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:NVOL refinement}.

In order to calculate the volume for a given partition $n$, consider a subset of boundary vertices where each vertex is part of the same partition. The outgoing communication volume associated with partition $n$ is then given by
\begin{equation} \label{partition_volume}
	\mathrm{partition\quad volume}[n] = \sum_{v \in V_b \mid P[v] = n} \left| N_v \right| w(v)
\end{equation}

We introduce a table $SG_i$, where each row corresponds to a source partition and each column to a destination partition for vertex $i$. The entry $SG_i[n][k]$ represents the gain contribution to partition $n$ when $i$ is moved to partition $k$. In a graph with $m$ partitions, a vertex \textit{i} to partition \textit{n} the total gain satisfies
\begin{equation}
	\sum_{t=0}^{m-1} SG_i[t][n] = G_i[n]
\end{equation}
i.e., the sum of each column in $SG_i$ equals the corresponding entry in $G_i$. For a candidate vertex \textit{i} being considered for a move, the partition-specific gain contributions to all possible destination partitions are evaluated for each neighbor $v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$ as follows:
\begin{enumerate} [label=\phantom{1.}]
	\item \textbf{Case 1.} \hypertarget{NVOL_case_1} If $P[v] = P[i]$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_{i-v}$ removes a shared inter-partition connection. This affects the source partition:
	\begin{equation}
		SG_i[P[i]][k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 2.} \hypertarget{NVOL_case_2} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] = 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_{iv}$ eliminates an inter-partition connection. This contributes to partition $P[v]$:
	\begin{equation} \label{case 2 nvol}
		SG_i[P[v]][k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}	
	\item \textbf{Case 3.} \hypertarget{NVOL_case_3} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] > 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_{i-v}$, introduces a new inter-partition connection. This contributes negatively to partition $P[v]$:
	\begin{equation}
		SG_i[P[v]][k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}	
	\item \textbf{Case 4.} \hypertarget{NVOL_case_4} If $C_i[P[i]] = 0$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in N_i$ yields an additional gain contribution for the destination partition:
	\begin{equation}
		SG_i[k][k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
It is also necessary to account for how both the source and destination partitions are affected by a vertex move. Although such a move redistributes communication volume between partitions, it does not alter the total communication volume of the graph. As discussed previously, NVOL requires precise tracking of how each move impacts individual partitions. When vertex $i$ is moved to partition $k$, this induces an additional gain contribution given by
\begin{align}
	SG_i[P[i]][k] &\label{eq:source_global_gain} \mathrel{+}= \left| N_i \right| \cdot w(i),\\
	SG_i[k][k]    &\label{eq:destination_global_gain}\mathrel{-}= \left| N_i \right| \cdot w(i).
\end{align}
This is referred to as a global term operation, as it reflects the overall redistribution of communication volume between the source and destination partitions of vertex $i$, rather than interactions with individual neighbor vertices. Equation~\ref{eq:source_global_gain} represents the volume leaving the source partition, while Equation~\ref{eq:destination_global_gain} the volume the destination receives. Consequently, it is computed once per vertex move, in contrast to the neighbor specific gain contributions described earlier, which are evaluated separately for each adjacent vertex. 

Apart from this distinction, both strategies follow the same case structure; the principal difference lies in how they store the gain information. Rather than recording only the magnitude of the change, NVOL also tracks which partition is affected. As it will be shown in Section~\ref{sec:NVOL refinement}, the gain table is essential for enforcing the partition-specif  logic used in NVOL, which permits moves that improves a single partition regardless of the total communication volume.

\section{Examples}
To better understand the definitions of communication volume and gain, we present a sequence of illustrative examples. Each example highlights a particular configuration and demonstrates how gain contributions arise under different vertex moves.
\subsection{Two Partition example}
Consider the graph in Figure~\ref{3}, consisting of five vertices with unit weights. The graph is partitioned into two subsets: the red partition containing vertices $\{A, B, C\}$, and the blue partition containing vertices $\{D, E\}$. Using Equation~\ref{total_volume}, the total communication volume is $4$.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\begin{minipage}[t]{.4\textwidth}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=.7\linewidth]{pictures/same_partition_example}
		\caption{Graph with a volume of 4, each partition has a volume of 2.}
		\label{3}
	\end{minipage}\qquad
	\begin{minipage}[t]{.4\textwidth}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=.7\linewidth]{pictures/same_partition_example_moved}
		\caption{Graph with a volume of 4, each partition has a volume of 2 after the move.}
		\label{4}
	\end{minipage}
\end{figure}

Vertex \textit{A} has one edge to the blue partition and contributes $1$ to the communication volume. Vertex \textit{B} has two edges incident to the blue partition, but contributes only $1$ under the given definition. Similarly, vertices \textit{D} and \textit{E} each contribute $1$. In contrast, vertex \textit{C} contributes nothing, as all its incident edges are internal to its partition.
	
Now consider moving vertex \textit{B} from the red partition to the blue partition, as shown in Figure~\ref{4}. We calculate the gain using the global strategy (Section~\ref{METIS gain calculation}). Let \textit{B} be the candidate vertex with neighbors $\{A, C, D, E\}$. Since there are two partitions, the gain vector $G_B$ has size two. In practice, however, $G_B$ effectively has size one, since $G_B[red]$ is always zero: a vertex moving from its source partition to the source partition is equivalent to no move at all.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{A}.}
Vertices \textit{A} and \textit{B} are in the same partition and both already have connections to the blue partition. Hence, the set $N_{B-A}$ is empty and no gain contribution arises.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{C}.}
Vertex \textit{C} lies in the red partition and has no edges to the blue partition. After moving \textit{B}, \textit{C} gains an inter-partition edge, increasing the communication volume. As shown in \hyperlink{METIS_case_1}{Case 1}, this yields a negative gain:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]
\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.}
Vertex \textit{D} is in the blue partition meaning that a move to the blue partition would decrease its volume only if one edge were incident on the red partition. Since \textit{D} has two, \hyperlink{METIS_case_3}{Case 3} does not apply, moving \textit{B} does not change its contribution, hence no case applies.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.}
Vertex \textit{E} satisfies \hyperlink{METIS_case_2}{Case 2}: it has exactly one connection to the red partition. After the move, this connection disappears, reducing the communication volume and yielding a positive gain:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]

\hyperlink{METIS_case_4}{Case 4} does not apply, as \textit{B} has multiple connections within its original partition. Summing all contributions yields total gain $0$, so the communication volume remains unchanged.

\paragraph{Partition-specific formulation.}
Using the formulation from Section~\ref{Partition specific gain calculation}, we obtain the same result. We use the table $SG_B$ with size $2\times 1$ (the source column will always be zeroed out just like the source in the gain vector), to store each volume change. As shown above only vertices \textit{C} and \textit{E} contribute:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 1, \quad
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]
Additionally, accounting for the direct effect of moving \textit{B} via Equations~\ref{eq:source_global_gain} and~\ref{eq:destination_global_gain}:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1, \quad
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]
The net effect is zero, confirming that neither total nor per-partition volume changes.
\subsection{Three partition example}
We now extend the example to three partitions. Consider the graph in Figure~\ref{three_partition_one_each}, where all vertices have unit weight. The partitions are: red $\{A,B\}$, blue $\{C,D\}$, and green $\{E\}$. The total communication volume is $8$, with partition volumes $3$, $3$, and $2$, respectively.
\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=.35\linewidth]{pictures/three_partition_one_each}
	\caption{Graph with a total communication volume of 8.}
	\label{three_partition_one_each}
\end{figure}
Let \textit{B} be the candidate vertex with neighbors $\{A,C,D,E\}$. Unlike the previous example, \textit{B} may move to either the blue or green partition. Thus, $G_B$ has size 3 and $SG_B$ $3\times 2$.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{A}.}
Vertex \textit{A} is in the same partition as \textit{B}. Since \textit{A} is connected to the blue partition but not the green partition, \hyperlink{METIS_case_1}{Case 1} applies only for a move to green, introducing a new inter-partition edge:
\[
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{C}.}
Vertex \textit{C} is outside the red partition and has exactly one edge to it. By \hyperlink{METIS_case_2}{Case 2}, removing this edge reduces the communication volume. Since \textit{C} is connected to both the blue and green partitions:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1, \quad
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.} Vertex \textit{D} has two edges to the red partition. By \hyperlink{METIS_case_3}{Case 3}, a gain change occurs only when the destination partition is not already adjacent to \textit{D}. This holds for the green partition:
\[
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.} Vertex \textit{E} is symmetrical to \textit{C}, yielding:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1, \quad
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]

Summing contributions gives:
\[
G_B = [0, 2, 0],
\]
indicating that moving \textit{B} to the blue partition reduces the total communication volume by $2$, while moving it to the green partition leaves it unchanged. 

\paragraph{Partition-specific formulation.}
The vector $G_B$ alone does not capture how the volume is redistributed among individual partitions. Using Section~\ref{Partition specific gain calculation}, we compute $SG_B$ as follows.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{A}.} Vertex \textit{A} follows \hyperlink{NVOL_case_1}{Case 1}for a move to the green partition, yielding:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{C}.} Vertex \textit{C} follows \hyperlink{NVOL_case_2}{Case 2} for both moves, yielding:
\begin{align*}
	SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.} Vertex \textit{D} falls under \hyperlink{NVOL_case_3}{Case 3} for the green partition, yielding: 
\[
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1,
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.} Vertex \textit{E} is symmetrical to \textit{C}, yielding:
\begin{align*}
	SG_B[\text{green}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_B[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}
Additionally, accounting for the direct effect of moving \textit{B} via Equations~\ref{eq:source_global_gain} and~\ref{eq:destination_global_gain}:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 2, \quad
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 2,
\]
\[
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 2, \quad
SG_B[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 2.
\]
The resulting table $SG_B$ is:
\[
\begin{blockarray}{ccc}
	& \text{blue} & \text{green} \\
	\begin{block}{c(cc)}
		\text{red}   & 2 & 1 \\
		\text{blue}  & -1 & 0 \\
		\text{green} & 1 & -1 \\
	\end{block}
\end{blockarray}
\]

Each column corresponds to a destination partition, and each row describes how a partition's volume changes. One can verify that the column sums recover $G_B$: the sums are 0 (the red partition is implied as no move occurs if the vertex stays in the same partition it already part of), $2+(-1)+1=2$ and $1+0+(-1)=0$ , consistent with $G_B = [0,2,0]$. This formulation provides finer insight: although moving \textit{B} to the green partition leaves the total volume unchanged, it redistributes volume across partitions. In particular, the blue partition experiences an increase in volume at the partition level.

\subsection{Case 4 example}
None of the preceding examples illustrate a scenario in which Case 4 is relevant. To clarify how such a case may arise and how it affects the communication volume, consider Figure~\ref{case4_example}, where all vertices carry unit weight. The partition assignment is as follows: red $\{A,B,C,G\}$, blue $\{D,E\}$, and green $\{F\}$. The total communication volume is $8$, with individual partition volumes of $3$, $3$, and $2$, respectively. As in the previous example, let $G_C$ have size 3 and $SG_C$  size $3\times 3$.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=.35\linewidth]{pictures/case4_example}
	\caption{Graph with a total communication volume of 8.}
	\label{case4_example}
\end{figure}

The candidate vertex is $C$, which has no internal edges within the red partition. Despite the graph containing additional vertices, only the direct neighbors $\{D, E, F\}$ of $C$ need to be visited in order to compute the gain. This illustrates how, even in large graphs, gain computation remains local to the immediate neighborhood of the moving vertex. For conciseness, both computation strategies are consolidated in this example, as they have been thoroughly demonstrated in earlier sections.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.} Vertex \textit{D} satisfies Case 2 for a move to the blue partition, yielding:
\begin{align*}
	G_C[\text{blue}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.} Vertex \textit{E} satisfies Case 2 for all possible moves of \textit{C}, yielding: 
\begin{align*}
	G_C[\text{blue}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	G_C[\text{green}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{F}.} Vertex $F$ has two edges connecting it to the red partition; however, the set $N_{C-F}$ is empty, meaning Case 3 does not apply and vertex $F$ contributes no change to the gain

\paragraph{Case 4.} Since vertex $C$ has no internal edges, any move will reduce the volume of its source partition. Under normal circumstances, when the candidate vertex has at least one internal edge, moving it leaves the receiving partition's incoming volume unchanged through the candidate. In this case, however, it is necessary to increment the gain for every possible destination partition, yielding:
\begin{align*}
 	G_C[\text{blue}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
 	G_C[\text{green}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
 	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
 	SG_C[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}
The resulting vector $G_C = [0\,3\,2]$. Accounting for the direct effect of relocating $C$ via Equations~\ref{eq:source_global_gain} and~\ref{eq:destination_global_gain}:
\[
SG_C[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 2, \quad
SG_C[\text{red}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 2,
\]
\[
SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 2, \quad
SG_C[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 2.
\]
The resulting table $SG_C$ is:
\[
\begin{blockarray}{ccc}
	& \text{blue} & \text{green} \\
	\begin{block}{c(cc)}
		\text{red}   & 2 & 2 \\
		\text{blue}  & 1 & 1 \\
		\text{green} & 0 & -1 \\
	\end{block}
\end{blockarray}
\]
Figures~\ref{case4_example_moved_blue} and~\ref{case4_example_moved_green} show the resulting graphs after moving \textit{C} to the blue and green partitions respectively. The reader can verify that the partition volumes in each case are consistent with the gains predicted by $SG_C$ and $G_C$. Without accounting for Case 4, the gain calculation would be incomplete, potentially leading to incorrect volume estimates when a vertex has no internal edges.

\section{Implementation}\label{cv_calc_implementation}
We now describe how the calculation of communication volume change is integrated into the multilevel framework. As mentioned previously, we have used METIS as the base on which to develop NVOL. The following description bridges the formal definitions above and the implemented code.

\subsection{Total gain implementation} \label{sec:global_gain_imp}
In Section~\ref{sec:preparation for refinement} the boundary set is computed using edge cut gain to filter and order it. When using total communication volume, we need to do the same thing using the calculations described in this chapter. Unlike the edge-cut metric, we cannot state for a given vertex whether moving it will lead to a positive gain or not. As stated in Section~\ref{sec:preparation for refinement}, we can deduce whether moving a vertex is guaranteed to give negative gain. This is not transferable to communication volume metrics.  

With communication volume, it is necessary to visit all neighbors of a vertex to correctly evaluate the gains of possible moves. This makes the calculation difficult to integrate directly into the preparation phase before refinement. In METIS, this issue was resolved by adding the gain calculation as a final step after preparation. Once the graph has been projected and all underlying structures have been recomputed, a full scan of the graph is performed to evaluate the possible gains for every vertex.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
	\caption{Total gain calculation}\label{alg:cap}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\State nparts $\gets k$ 							\Comment{Graph with $k$ partitions}
		\ForAll{$i \in V$}
		\If{$|N_i|>0$}										\Comment{Only visit vertices with neighboring partitions}
		\ForAll{$v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$}					\Comment{Visit all adjacent vertices of i}
		\For{n = 0; n < nparts}
		\If{n $\in N_i$ \textbf{AND} n $\in N_v$}
		\State $N_{iv}$.add(n)					\Comment{Shared partitions between \textit{i} and \textit{v} ($N_{iv}$)}
		\ElsIf{n $\in N_i$ \textbf{AND} n $\notin N_v$}
		\State $N_{i-v}$.add(n)					\Comment{Partitions only \textit{i} is connected to ($N_{i-v}$)}
		\EndIf
		\EndFor
		
		\If{$P[i] = P[v]$}
		\ForAll{$n \in N_{i-v}$}
		\State $G[n] \mathrel{-}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 1}
		\EndFor
		\ElsIf{$P[i] \mathrel{!}= P[v]$ \textbf{AND} $C_v[P[i]] = 1$}	
		\ForAll{$n \in N_{iv}$}
		\State $G[n] \mathrel{+}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 2}
		\EndFor
		\ElsIf{$P[i] \mathrel{!}= P[v]$ \textbf{AND} $C_v[P[i]] > 1$}
		\ForAll{$n \in N_{i-v}$}
		\State $G[n] \mathrel{-}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 3}
		\EndFor
		\EndIf
		\EndFor	
		\If{$C_i[P[i]] = 0$}
		\ForAll{$n \in N_i$}
		\State $G[n] \mathrel{+}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 4}
		\EndFor
		\EndIf
		\State BoundarySet.add(MAX($G_v$))			\Comment{Order by highest possible gain}
		\EndIf
		\EndFor	
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Partition specific gain implementation} \label{sec:partition specific gain imp}
The implementation of the partition-specific gain calculation required a different approach, even though the logic in the inner loop is very similar. Before starting refinement, METIS calculates and stores the gain of all boundary vertices. However, NVOL requires significantly more memory: for a gain vector of 10 elements, NVOL's gain table contains 90 entries. For this reason, we opted for an on-the-fly approach, computing the gain table for each vertex as it is visited during refinement. Beyond the memory overhead, a precomputed gain table would also require an update procedure to maintain consistency after each move. As this is not directly relevant to the research questions of this thesis, we leave this as future work, discussed further in Chapter~\ref{chap_future_work}.

NVOL is implemented directly in the refinement stage. During preparation, it uses the same structure as Section~\ref{sec:global_gain_imp}, ordering vertices based on their total gain. Once refinement begins, the algorithm visits every vertex in the boundary set. For each vertex $i$ under consideration, we run the algorithm below:
\begin{algorithm}[h]
	\caption{Partition specific gain calculation}\label{alg:nvol}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\State nparts $\gets k$ 							\Comment{Graph with $k$ partitions}
		\ForAll{$v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$}					\Comment{Visit all adjacent vertices of i}
		\For{n = 0; n < nparts}
		\If{n $\in N_i$ \textbf{AND} n $\in N_v$}
		\State $N_{iv}$.add(n)					\Comment{Shared partitions between \textit{i} and \textit{v} ($N_{iv}$)}
		\ElsIf{n $\in N_i$ \textbf{AND} n $\notin N_v$}
		\State $N_{i-v}$.add(n)					\Comment{Partitions only \textit{i} is connected to ($N_{i-v}$)}
		\EndIf
		\EndFor
		
		\If{$P[i] = P[v]$}
		\ForAll{$n \in N_{i-v}$}
		\State $SG[P[v]][n] \mathrel{-}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 1}
		\EndFor
		\ElsIf{$P[i] \mathrel{!}= P[v]$ \textbf{AND} $C_v[P[i]] = 1$}	
		\ForAll{$n \in N_{iv}$}
		\State $SG[P[v]][n] \mathrel{+}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 2}
		\EndFor
		\ElsIf{$P[i] \mathrel{!}= P[v]$ \textbf{AND} $C_v[P[i]] > 1$}
		\ForAll{$n \in N_{i-v}$}
		\State $SG[P[v]][n] \mathrel{-}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 3}
		\EndFor
		\EndIf
		\EndFor	
		\If{$C_i[P[i]] = 0$}
		\ForAll{$n \in N_i$}
		\State $SG[n][n] \mathrel{+}= w(i)$				\Comment{Case 4}
		\EndFor
		\EndIf
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
