\chapter{Communication Volume Calculation}
To support the new optimization objective for NVOL, it is necessary to introduce a new procedure for calculating communication volume. The existing implementation in METIS focuses on optimizing the total communication volume between partitions. Therefore it only evaluates the net change in total communication volume after a potential move, instead of how each partition volume changes by the move. 
%While this representation is sufficient for global optimization, it does not provide the partition specific information required by the proposed optimization strategy.
In contrast, the optimization approach introduced in this work delivers detailed information about how individual partitions are affected by a potential move. 
%To address this requirement, a matrix based data structure was developed. In this representation, each row corresponds to a partition, while each column represents a potential destination partition. Each matrix element therefore captures the gain associated with a specific partition when a move to a particular destination is considered.

%The total gain for a move can still be derived by summing the values within a column, which yields the same aggregate gain measure used by METIS. However, unlike the original approach, this representation preserves the contribution to each individual partition. Consequently, it enables partition aware optimization decisions by explicitly revealing how each partition is affected by a move.

\section{Basic definitions}
To facilitate understanding of the implementation (see Section~\ref{cv_calc_implementation}), we begin by introducing a formal definition of communication volume. Let $G = (V, E)$ be a graph, and let $P$ be the partition vector, where $P[v]$ is the partition a vertex \textit{v} is assigned to. A vertex $u$ is defined as a neighbor of $v$ if there exists an edge $\{u,v\} \in E$. Any vertex adjacent to at least one vertex belonging to a different partition is said to lie on the boundary. We denote the set of all boundary vertices by $V_b \subseteq V$. Formally, a vertex $v \in V_b$ if there exists at least one neighbor $u \in V$ such that $\{v,u\} \in E$ and $P[v] \neq P[u]$.

For each boundary vertex $v \in V_b$, let $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v$ denote the set of distinct partitions (excluding $P[v]$) hosting its neighboring vertices. The number of partitions contained in $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v$, defined as $\lvert V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v \rvert$, is the outgoing communication volume of vertex $v$. Note that the number of edges incident to $v$ is not directly relevant for this measure, only the number of distinct neighboring partitions. In Figure~\ref{1}, vertex $A$ has a communication volume of 2, as it is connected to vertices in two distinct partitions. In contrast, Figure~\ref{2} illustrates a case where vertex $A$ has two edges, both connecting to vertices within the same partition. Consequently, the communication volume of vertex $A$ is 1, since only a single distinct external partition is involved.

The total communication volume of the partitioned graph is obtained by summing the outgoing volumes over all boundary vertices:
\begin{equation} \label{total_volume}
	\mathrm{total\_volume} = \sum_{v \in V_b} \left| V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v \right|\mathrm{w(v)}
\end{equation}
Here, $w(v)$ denotes the weight associated with vertex $v$, which scales its contribution to the outgoing communication volume. In Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example}, all vertices are assumed to have a unit weight. The boundary set is $V_b = \{A, B, C, D, E\}$. Note that vertex \textit{F} is not part of this set, as it has no edges connecting to vertices in other partitions.

Applying Equation~\ref{total_volume}, the total communication volume is computed by summing the contributions of all vertices in the boundary set. Vertices \textit{A}, \textit{C}, and \textit{D} each contribute a volume of 2, while vertices \textit{B} and \textit{E} each contribute a volume of 1. This results in a total communication volume of 8.

\section{Global gain calculation} \label{METIS gain calculation}
To determine which vertices should be moved, and to which partitions, METIS evaluates the gain associated with each potential move. The gain represents the change in total communication volume resulting from relocating a vertex to a new partition. A positive gain indicates a reduction in total communication volume, whereas a negative gain corresponds to an increase.

Since communication only arises when an edge connects two vertices belonging to different partitions, a vertex move can only affect the communication volume of its neighboring partitions. For each boundary vertex $i \in V_b$, METIS examines all its adjacent vertices $v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$, where $V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i \subseteq V$ denotes the set of vertices adjacent to $i$. When considering moving a boundary vertex \textit{i}, the gain calculation depends on the relationship that \textit{i} and each of its neighboring vertices have with other partitions. The effect on volume depends on which partitions they share. In particular, we define for every vertex \textit{i} and every neighbor vertex \textit{v}
\begin{equation}
	V^{i}_{iv} = V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_i \setminus (V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v \cup P[v])
	\quad \text{and} \quad
	V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{iv} = (V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_i \cap V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v) \cup P[v]
\end{equation}
representing partitions exclusive to $i$ and partitions shared by $i$ and $v$, respectively. In both cases, we add in $P[v]$ as this partition is shared even though it is not part of the $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v$ set. A vertex \textit{v} has access to its own partition $P[v]$, meaning that $P[v]$ needs to be removed in $V^{i}_{iv}$ and added in $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{iv}$ to precisely determine shared partitions. Notice how both sets are subsets of $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_i = V^{i}_{iv} \cup V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{iv}$.

As a concrete example, consider Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example} and suppose the candidate vertex is~$A$, with neighbor~$B$ under consideration. Here, $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_A = \{\mathrm{blue}, \mathrm{green}\}$, while $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_B = \{\mathrm{blue}\}$. Consequently, $V^{A}_{AB} = \{\text{green}\}$, since green is the only partition connected to~$A$ but not to~$B$, whereas $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{AB} = \{\mathrm{blue}\}$ as both vertices have at least one edge to the blue partition. Keeping \textit{A} as the candidate vertex, consider now neighbor~$C$ with $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_C = \{\mathrm{red}, \mathrm{green}\}$. \textit{A} and \textit{C} have access to the same partitions, meaning that $V^{A}_{AC} = \emptyset$ and $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{AC} = \{\mathrm{blue}, \mathrm{green}\}$. This example highlights the need for adding $P[v]$ to the sets. The sets would have been $V^{A}_{AC} = \{\mathrm{blue}\}$, $V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{AC} = \{\mathrm{green}\}$ if we had not accounted for $P[C]$. However, by simply viewing Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example} we can see that the blue partition is shared and not unique to \textit{A}.

Let $C_i[n]$ denote the number of edges connecting $i$ to partition $n$. In Figure~\ref{tot_vol_example}, for example, $C_A[\mathrm{blue}] = 1$, as \textit{A} has only one edge connected to the blue partition, while $C_D[\mathrm{blue}] = 2$, as \textit{D} has two edges connected to the blue partition. Let $G_i$ be a vector with a number of elements equal to the number of partitions, initialized at 0, where $G_i[n]$ represents the total gain obtained by moving vertex $i$ to partition $n$. This structure is used by METIS to store for each candidate node, the resulting change of moving to a partition. For a candidate vertex \textit{i} being considered for a move, the gain contributions to all possible destination partitions are evaluated for each neighbor $v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$ as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\phantom{1.}]\label{METIS CASES}
	\item \textbf{Case 1.} \label{METIS case 1}If $P[v] = P[i]$, then moving $i$ to any partition in $k \in V^i_{iv}$ creates a shared inter-partition connection for $v$, yielding
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 2.}\label{METIS case 2} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] = 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{iv}$ eliminates an inter-partition connection for $v$, yielding 
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 3.}\label{METIS case 3} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] > 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^i_{iv}$ introduces a new inter-partition connection for $v$, resulting in a gain contribution of 
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 4.} \label{METIS case 4} If $C_i[P[i]] = 0$, i.e., $i$ has no internal degree, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^{nbr}_i$ yields an additional gain contribution of
	\begin{equation}
		G_i[k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
For every adjacent boundary vertex $v$ that it visits, METIS will check each of the cases above for every partition $k \in V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_i$. The algorithm below displays the flow of how METIS evaluates the gain for a boundary vertex:
%ADD PSEUDOCODE HERE
\begin{algorithm}
	\caption{Global gain calculation}\label{alg:cap}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\State $partition_i \gets P[i]$
		\State $Nbr_i \gets i.nbr\textunderscore partitions$	\Comment{Adjacent partitions of \textit{i}}
		\State $Adj \gets i.adj\textunderscore vertices$		\Comment{Adjacent vertices of \textit{i}}
		\State $C_i \gets i.connections$ 						\Comment{Vector where each element represents the edges \textit{i} has to a partition}
		\State $vsize \gets i.size$
		\ForAll{$v \in Adj$}
			\State $partition_v \gets P[v]$
			\State $Nbr_v \gets v.nbr\textunderscore partitions$
			\State $Nbr_v$.add($P[v]$)
			\State $C_v \gets v.connections$ 
			
			\For{n = 0; n < nparts}
				\If{n in $Nbr_i$ AND n in $Nbr_v$}
					\State $V_{nbr}$.add(n)					\Comment{Shared partitions between \textit{i} and \textit{v} ($V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{iv}$)}
				\ElsIf{n in $Nbr_i$ AND n NOT IN $Nbr_v$}
					\State $V_{i}$.add(n)					\Comment{Partitions only \textit{i} is connected to ($V^{i}_{iv}$)}
				\EndIf
			\EndFor
			
			\If{$partition_i = partition_v$}
				\ForAll{$n \in V_i$}
					\State $G[n] \mathrel{-}= vsize$
				\EndFor
			\ElsIf{$partition_i \mathrel{!}= partition_v$ AND $C_v[partition_i] = 1$}	
				\ForAll{$n \in V_{nbr}$}
					\State $G[n] \mathrel{+}= vsize$
				\EndFor
			\ElsIf{$partition_i \mathrel{!}= partition_v$ AND $C_v[partition_i] > 1$}
				\ForAll{$n \in V_{i}$}
					\State $G[n] \mathrel{-}= vsize$
				\EndFor
			\EndIf
			
			\If{$C_i[partition_i] = 1$}
				\ForAll{$n \in Nbr_i$}
					\State $G[n] \mathrel{+}= vsize$
				\EndFor
			\EndIf
		\EndFor		
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
\section{Partition specific gain calculation} \label{Partition specific gain calculation}
To analyze the communication volume at the partition level, we need to expand the strategy used by METIS as shown in Section~\ref{METIS gain calculation}. By only having the global gain for a move, information regarding how this gain affected each partition is missing. 

In order to calculate the volume for a given partition $n$, consider a subset of boundary vertices where each vertex is part of the same partition. The communication volume associated with partition $n$ is then given by
\begin{equation} \label{partition_volume}
	\mathrm{partition\_volume}_n = \sum_{v \in V_b \mid P[v] = n} \left| V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_v \right| w(v)
\end{equation}

We introduce a table $SG_i$, where each row corresponds to a source partition and each column to a destination partition for vertex $i$. The entry $SG_i[n][k]$ represents the gain contribution to partition $n$ when $i$ is moved to partition $k$. In a graph with $m$ partitions, a vertex \textit{i} to partition \textit{n} the total gain satisfies
\begin{equation}
	\sum_{t=0}^{m-1} SG_i[t][n] = G_i[n]
\end{equation}
i.e., the sum of each column in $SG_i$ equals the corresponding entry in $SG_i$. For a candidate vertex \textit{i} being considered for a move, the partition-specific gain contributions to all possible destination partitions are evaluated for each neighbor $v \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_i$ as follows:
\begin{enumerate} [label=\phantom{1.}]
	\item \textbf{Case 1.} \label{NVOL case 1} If $P[v] = P[i]$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^i_{iv}$ removes a shared inter-partition connection. This affects the source partition:
	\begin{equation}
		SG_i[P[i]][k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
	\item \textbf{Case 2.} \label{NVOL case 2} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] = 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_{iv}$ eliminates an inter-partition connection. This contributes to partition $P[v]$:
	\begin{equation} \label{case 2 nvol}
		SG_i[P[v]][k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}	
	\item \textbf{Case 3.} \label{NVOL case 3} If $P[v] \neq P[i]$ and $C_v[P[i]] > 1$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^i_{iv}$, introduces a new inter-partition connection. This contributes negatively to partition $P[v]$:
	\begin{equation}
		SG_i[P[v]][k] \mathrel{-}= w(i)
	\end{equation}	
	\item \textbf{Case 4.} \label{NVOL case 4} If $C_i[P[i]] = 0$, then moving $i$ to any partition $k \in V^{nbr}_i$ yields an additional gain contribution for the destination partition:
	\begin{equation}
		SG_i[k][k] \mathrel{+}= w(i)
	\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
It is also necessary to account for how both the source and destination partitions are affected by a vertex move. Although such a move redistributes communication volume between partitions, it does not alter the total communication volume of the graph. As discussed previously, NVOL requires precise tracking of how each move impacts individual partitions. When vertex $i$ is moved to partition $k$, this induces an additional gain contribution given by
\begin{align}
	SG_i[P[i]][k] &\mathrel{+}= \left| V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_i \right| \cdot w(i), \label{global_term_from} \\
	SG_i[k][k]    &\mathrel{-}= \left| V^{\mathrm{nbr}}_i \right| \cdot w(i). \label{global_term_dest}
\end{align}
This is referred to as a global term operation, as it reflects the overall redistribution of communication volume between the source and destination partitions of vertex $i$, rather than interactions with individual neighbor vertices. Consequently, it is computed once per vertex move, in contrast to the neighbor specific gain contributions described earlier, which are evaluated separately for each adjacent vertex.

Apart from this distinction, both strategies follow the same case structure; the principal difference lies in how they store the gain information. Rather than recording only the magnitude of the change, NVOL also tracks which partition is affected.

\section{Examples}
To better understand the definitions of communication volume and gain, we present a sequence of illustrative examples. Each example highlights a particular configuration and demonstrates how gain contributions arise under different vertex moves.
\subsection{Two Partition example}
Consider the graph in Figure~\ref{3}, consisting of five vertices with unit weights. The graph is partitioned into two subsets: the red partition containing vertices $\{A, B, C\}$, and the blue partition containing vertices $\{D, E\}$. Using Equation~\ref{total_volume}, the total communication volume is $4$.

		\centering

		\caption{Graph with a volume of 4, each partition has a volume of 2 after the move.}
		\label{4}
	\end{minipage}
\end{figure}

Vertex \textit{A} has one edge to the blue partition and contributes $1$ to the communication volume. Vertex \textit{B} has two edges incident to the blue partition, but contributes only $1$ under the given definition. Similarly, vertices \textit{D} and \textit{E} each contribute $1$. In contrast, vertex \textit{C} contributes nothing, as all its incident edges are internal to its partition.
	
Now consider moving vertex \textit{B} from the red partition to the blue partition, as shown in Figure~\ref{4}. We calculate the gain using the METIS strategy (Section~\ref{METIS gain calculation}). Let \textit{B} be the candidate vertex with neighbors $\{A, C, D, E\}$. Since there are two partitions, the gain vector $G_B \in \mathbb{R}^2$ has size two.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{A}.}
Vertices \textit{A} and \textit{B} are in the same partition and both already have connections to the blue partition. Hence, the set $M$ is empty and no gain contribution arises.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{C}.}
Vertex \textit{C} lies in the red partition and has no edges to the blue partition. After moving \textit{B}, \textit{C} gains an inter-partition edge, increasing the communication volume. As shown in Case~\ref{METIS case 1}, this yields a negative gain:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]
\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.}
Vertex \textit{D} is in the blue partition meaning that a move to the blue partition would decrease its volume only if one edge were incident on the red partition. Since \textit{D} has two,  Case~\ref{METIS case 3} does not apply, moving \textit{B} does not change its contribution, hence no case applies.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.}
Vertex \textit{E} satisfies Case~\ref{METIS case 2}: it has exactly one connection to the red partition. After the move, this connection disappears, reducing the communication volume and yielding a positive gain:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]

Case~\ref{METIS case 4} does not apply, as \textit{B} has multiple connections within its original partition. Summing all contributions yields total gain $0$, so the communication volume remains unchanged.

\paragraph{Partition-specific formulation.}
Using the formulation from Section~\ref{Partition specific gain calculation}, we obtain the same result. We use the table $SG_B \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}$ to store each volume change. As shown above only vertices \textit{C} and \textit{E} contribute:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 1, \quad
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]
Additionally, accounting for the direct effect of moving \textit{B} via Equations~\ref{global_term_from} and~\ref{global_term_dest}:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1, \quad
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]
The net effect is zero, confirming that neither total nor per-partition volume changes.

We now extend the example to three partitions. Consider the graph in Figure~\ref{three_partition_one_each}, where all vertices have unit weight. The partitions are: red $\{A,B\}$, blue $\{C,D\}$, and green $\{E\}$. The total communication volume is $8$, with partition volumes $3$, $3$, and $2$, respectively.

Let \textit{B} be the candidate vertex with neighbors $\{A,C,D,E\}$. Unlike the previous example, \textit{B} may move to either the blue or green partition. Thus, $G_B \in \mathbb{R}^3$ and $SG_B \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 3}$.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{A}.}
Vertex \textit{A} is in the same partition as \textit{B}. Since \textit{A} is connected to the blue partition but not the green partition, Case~\ref{METIS case 1} applies only for a move to green, introducing a new inter-partition edge:
\[
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{C}.}
Vertex \textit{C} is outside the red partition and has exactly one edge to it. By Case~\ref{METIS case 2}, removing this edge reduces the communication volume. Since \textit{C} is connected to both the blue and green partitions:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1, \quad
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.} Vertex \textit{D} has two edges to the red partition. By Case~\ref{METIS case 3}, a gain change occurs only when the destination partition is not already adjacent to \textit{D}. This holds for the green partition:
\[
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1.
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.} Vertex \textit{E} is symmetrical to \textit{C}, yielding:
\[
G_B[\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1, \quad
G_B[\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1.
\]

Summing contributions gives:
\[
G_B = [0, 2, 0],
\]
indicating that moving \textit{B} to the blue partition reduces the total communication volume by $2$, while moving it to the green partition leaves it unchanged. Here 

\paragraph{Partition-specific formulation.}
The vector $G_B$ alone does not capture how the volume is redistributed among individual partitions. Using Section~\ref{Partition specific gain calculation}, we compute $SG_B$ as follows.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{A}.} Vertex \textit{A} follows Case~\ref{NVOL case 1} for a move to the green partition, yielding:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{C}.} Vertex \textit{C} follows Case~\ref{NVOL case 2} for both moves, yielding:
\begin{align*}
	SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.} Vertex \textit{D} falls under Case~\ref{NVOL case 3} for the green partition, yielding: 
\[
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 1,
\]

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.} Vertex \textit{E} is symmetrical to \textit{C}, yielding:
\begin{align*}
	SG_B[\text{green}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_B[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}
Additionally, accounting for the direct effect of moving \textit{B} via Equations~\ref{global_term_from} and~\ref{global_term_dest}:
\[
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 2, \quad
SG_B[\text{red}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 2,
\]
\[
SG_B[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 2, \quad
SG_B[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 2.
\]
The resulting table $SG_B$ is:
\[
\begin{blockarray}{cccc}
	& \text{red} & \text{blue} & \text{green} \\
	\begin{block}{c(ccc)}
		\text{red}   & 0 & 2 & 1 \\
		\text{blue}  & 0 & -1 & 0 \\
		\text{green} & 0 & 1 & -1 \\
	\end{block}
\end{blockarray}
\]

Each column corresponds to a destination partition, and each row describes how a partition's volume changes. One can verify that the column sums recover $G_B$: the sums are $0+0+0=0$, $2+(-1)+1=2$, and $1+0+(-1)=0$, consistent with $G_B = [0,2,0]$. This formulation provides finer insight: although moving \textit{B} to the green partition leaves the total volume unchanged, it redistributes volume across partitions. In particular, the blue partition experiences an increase in volume at the partition level.

\subsection{Case 4 example}
None of the preceding examples illustrate a scenario in which Case 4 is relevant. To clarify how such a case may arise and how it affects the communication volume, consider Figure~\ref{case4_example}, where all vertices carry unit weight. The partition assignment is as follows: red $\{A,B,C,D\}$, blue $\{D,E\}$, and green $\{F\}$. The total communication volume is $8$, with individual partition volumes of $3$, $3$, and $2$, respectively. As in the previous example, let $G_C \in \mathbb{R}^3$ and $SG_C \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 3}$.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=.35\linewidth]{pictures/case4_example}
	\caption{Graph with a total communication volume of 8.}
	\label{case4_example}
\end{figure}

The candidate vertex is $C$, which has no internal edges within the red partition. Despite the graph containing additional vertices, only the direct neighbors $\{D, E, F\}$ of $C$ need to be visited in order to compute the gain. This illustrates how, even in large graphs, gain computation remains local to the immediate neighborhood of the moving vertex. For conciseness, both computation strategies are consolidated in this example, as they have been thoroughly demonstrated in earlier sections.

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{D}.} Vertex \textit{D} satisfies Case 2 for a move to the blue partition, yielding:
\begin{align*}
	G_C[\text{blue}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{E}.} Vertex \textit{E} satisfies Case 2 for all possible moves of \textit{C}, yielding: 
\begin{align*}
	G_C[\text{blue}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	G_C[\text{green}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Vertex \textit{F}.} Vertex $F$ has two edges connecting it to the red partition; however, the set $M$ is empty, meaning Case 3 does not apply and vertex $F$ contributes no change to the gain

\paragraph{Case 4.} Since vertex $C$ has no internal edges, any move will reduce the volume of its source partition. Under normal circumstances, when the candidate vertex has at least one internal edge, moving it leaves the receiving partition's incoming volume unchanged through the candidate. In this case, however, it is necessary to increment the gain for every possible destination partition, yielding:
\begin{align*}
 	G_C[\text{blue}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
 	G_C[\text{green}]\mathrel{+}= 1 \\
 	SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 1 \\
 	SG_C[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 1
\end{align*}
The resulting vector $G_C = [0\,3\,2]$. Accounting for the direct effect of relocating $C$ via Equations~\ref{global_term_from} and~\ref{global_term_dest}:
\[
SG_C[\text{red}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{+}= 2, \quad
SG_C[\text{red}][\text{green}] \mathrel{+}= 2,
\]
\[
SG_C[\text{blue}][\text{blue}] \mathrel{-}= 2, \quad
SG_C[\text{green}][\text{green}] \mathrel{-}= 2.
\]
The resulting table $SG_C$ is:
\[
\begin{blockarray}{cccc}
	& \text{red} & \text{blue} & \text{green} \\
	\begin{block}{c(ccc)}
		\text{red}   & 0 & 2 & 2 \\
		\text{blue}  & 0 & 1 & 1 \\
		\text{green} & 0 & 1 & -1 \\
	\end{block}
\end{blockarray}
\]
Figures~\ref{case4_example_moved_blue} and~\ref{case4_example_moved_green} show the resulting graphs after moving \textit{C} to the blue and green partitions respectively. The reader can verify that the partition volumes in each case are consistent with the gains predicted by $SG_C$ and $G_C$. Without accounting for Case 4, the gain calculation would be incomplete, potentially leading to incorrect volume estimates when a vertex has no internal edges.
