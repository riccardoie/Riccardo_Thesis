\chapter{Multilevel K-Way Graph Partitioning}\label{chap:multilevel-kway}
This chapter presents the Multilevel K-way Graph Partitioning algorithm which is one of the most widespread GP algorithms. Each section will explain in detail the phases of the multilevel approach with special focus on the techniques used in the partitioner used in this thesis.  

\section{Preliminaries}
The overall structure of a multilevel k-way graph partitioning algorithm is fairly straightforward. The process begins by coarsening the input graph until it is reduced to a size of only a few thousand vertices. At this coarsest level, an initial k-way partitioning is computed using
a graph growing algorithm (see Section~\ref{graph_growing})~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. The graph is then uncoarsened back to its original size in successive steps. At each level of uncoarsening, the partitioning is refined to improve quality by exploiting the increased degrees of freedom available at the finer level. Karypis and Kumar demonstrate in~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998} that this multilevel approach yields high quality partitions, often comparable to, or better than, those produced by recursive bisection~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}.

In Figure~\ref{fig:multilevel-overview}, this process is illustrated, showing how an initial graph $G_0$ is coarsened until it reaches $G_4$,  which is smaller in size. At this level, an initial partition is performed on the coarsest graph. After that, the graph is step by step projected back to its original size, refining the partition at each level in order to minimize the chosen objective function~\cite{KarypisGeorge1999PMkP}.

The multilevel partitioning problem uses the same definitions as in Section~\ref{sec:gpp}. Additionally, we represent a k-way partition through a vector $P$. The vector has a length of $n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices in the graph. Each element of \textit{P} is an integer between 1 and $k$, indicating the partition the vertex belongs to. Specifically, a vertex $v \in V$ assigned to partition \textit{a} will have $P[v] = a$. The next sections will now explain how each phase works and how the partitions are formed.

\section{Coarsening Phase}
During the coarsening phase, a sequence of increasingly smaller graphs is constructed, maintaining a similar structure to the original graph with fewer degrees of freedom. The most common approach is to aggregate groups of vertices into a single coarse vertex through matching~\cite{buluc_recent_2016, KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. Formally, the idea is to contract pairs of matched vertices, where we define a matching as a set of edges no two of which share a vertex~\cite{buluc_recent_2016, KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. Generally, a matching is considered good if it contains many edges. To achieve this, \textit{maximal matchings} are used. A matching is maximal if any edge in the graph that is not contained in the matching has at least one of its endpoints matched~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. The following subsection describes in detail the implementation of Sorted Heavy Edge Matching (SHEM), which is the matching algorithm employed by the partitioner used in this thesis. For further reading on other coarsening algorithms, we refer the reader to~\cite{buluc_recent_2016, KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. 

Once a matching is performed the coarse vertex has a weight equal to the sum of the weights of all the vertices it contains. Additionally, the coarse vertex receives all the edges of the vertices it contains. Vertices that are not matched are simply copied over to the smaller graph. This process is repeated until we reach a graph $G_m$ with a small enough number of vertices~\cite{karypis_analysis_1995}.

For a given graph $G_i = (V_i, E_i)$, a subset of vertices $V^v_i \subseteq V_i$ is merged into a coarse vertex $v \in V_{i+1}$ for the next coarser graph $G_{i+1}$. The coarse vertex $v$ has weight $w(v) = \sum_{u \in V^v_i} w(u)$. This ensures that the total vertex weight is preserved across coarsening levels. To preserve connectivity, the new vertex $v$ receives the union of the edges of the vertices in $V^v_i$. For any neighboring vertex $u$, the edge weight is $w(\{v,u\}) = \sum_{z\in V^v_i}w(\{z,u\})$. This aggregation strategy preserves structural and weight-related properties of the original graph, thereby enabling efficient and balanced processing at coarser levels~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}.

\subsection{Sorted Heavy Edge Matching}
SHEM begins by generating a random permutation of the graph's vertices, which is then sorted based on vertex degrees. Each vertex gets assigned a bucket based on the square of its degree, sorted from lowest to highest. This ordering ensures that low-degree vertices, which have fewer opportunities for matching, are prioritized and thus more likely to be successfully matched. Delaying them would increase the chance of remaining unmatched. Once the vertices are sorted, they are traversed in order. For each unmatched vertex $u$, the algorithm selects the unmatched neighboring vertex $v$ connected to $u$ via the heaviest incident edge. The edge $\{u,v\}$ is then added to the maximal matching $M_i$, and both $u$ and $v$ are marked as matched. If no suitable neighbor exists, the vertex $u$ remains unmatched. This process continues until all vertices have been processed.

The greedy nature of SHEM tends to preserve strong edge connections in the coarser graph, contributing to a significant reduction in total edge weight and, consequently, in the number of edge cuts~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH, karypis_parallel_1999}. As shown in \cite{karypis_analysis_1995} by Karypis and Kumar, since the coarser graph has lower edge weight, it also has a lower edge cut. 

\section{Initial Partitioning Phase}
Once the graph has been coarsened to the smallest level, the initial partitioning is performed. The goal of this stage is to produce $k$ roughly equal partitions before further refinement. Several partitioning algorithms can be used here, including spectral partitioning~\cite{hendrickson_multilevel_1995}, graph growing partitioning~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}, and multilevel recursive bisection~\cite{simon_recursive_bisection}, among others. Since the graph is relatively small at this stage, the partitioning is fast. Our partitioner, METIS, uses a multilevel recursive bisection approach. It is also worth mentioning that METIS uses different bisection algorithms based on the graph type. Using the definition in Section~\ref{sec:gpp} we use GGP as the default bisection algorithm.

The a multilevel recursive bisection approach takes the coarse graph and bisects it using GGP. The resulting two subgraphs are then each bisected again. This process is repeated recursively until the desired number of partitions is reached. When the number of partitions is not a power of 2, the algorithm will keep bisecting until one graph has 3 partition remaining. At this level only one of the produced subgraphs will be further bisected. Consider Figure~\ref{fig:tree} where graph $G$ is to be partitioned into 7 groups. At the first level, $G_0$ has 4 partitions remaining while $G_1$ has 3. Since $G_1$ has only 3 partitions remaining, only $G_4$ is bisected further. The left side ($G_0$), on the other hand, continues bisecting until all partitions have been produced.

During each bisecting step, GGP is used. As it was described in Section~\ref{graph_growing}, this bisection approach bases itself on BFS. Starting from a random seed it tries to group together vertices connected to each other. At every step it checks that both partitions maintain a weight threshold. Once a partition is computed the graph is further refined using the refinement algorithm proposed by Fiduccia and Mattheyses (FM) in \cite{fiduccia_fm}. The FM refinement algorithm goes through the boundary vertices of the graph, vertices that share at least one edge with a vertex in the other partition. For each step the vertex move that yields the greatest reduction in edge cut (see Section~\ref{subs:objective_functions}) is selected. In order to avoid a subpar solution, FM allows a limited number of moves that worsen the edge cut. This process is repeated over the boundary vertices several times, each time keeping the partition with the lowest edge cut.

It is worth noting that also for our volume based objective we have made use of this approach based on edge cut for the initial partition. There are a few reasons for this. Firstly, the initial partition is going to be heavily refined later during the uncoarsening phase, making the quality of the initial partition not as important. Additionally algorithms like FM are fast and work well with edge cut, optimizing for another objective would make this phase significantly slower. All of these reasons make it reasonable to use edge cut as an objective for this first partitioning.  

\section{Uncoarsening Phase} \label{sec:uncoarsening}
The final step is the uncoarsening and refinement phase, during which the graph is gradually projected back to its original size. The coarsest graph $G_m$ is successively expanded through finer representations $G_{m-1}, G_{m-2}, \dots, G_1$. At each projection from $G_{i+1}$ to $G_i$, every vertex contained within a coarse vertex $v$ inherits the partition assignment that $v$ held in $G_{i+1}$. Once all coarse vertices at a given level have been expanded, the resulting partition is improved using local refinement heuristics.

Depending on the objective function chosen, the refinement heuristics will be different. In the rest of this section, we present the standard approach used by our partitioner, METIS, based on edge cut. The edge cut based refinement is simpler to understand, and will help the reader to get a better idea of how refinement works. In Chapter~\ref{cv_calculation} and Chapter~\ref{chapter5} we will discuss the communication volume objective and present our new and expanded objective NVOL.

METIS employs a variant of the Kernighan-Lin (KL) algorithm \cite{Kernighan_kl} to refine the graph. The KL algorithm iteratively swaps vertices among partitions of a bisection to minimize edge cut, continuing until no further improvement is possible and a local minimum is reached~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. Refining a k-way partition is significantly more complex since each vertex has $k - 1$ possible moves as opposed to just one in a bisection\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. This substantially enlarges the optimization space, forcing the algorithm to calculate the gain in each possible combination, thus increasing the computational complexity. The gain represents the change in total edge cut resulting from relocating a vertex to a new partition.

\subsection{Preparation before refinement}\label{sec:preparation for refinement}
At every level of uncoarsening the graph undergoes a preparation phase before actual refinement. The new finer graph contains new vertices and edges, requiring a recomputation of the underlying structures describing the graph. During this phase the whole graph is therefore traversed in order to get accurate information of the new connections in the graph. Along this stage, the boundary set is also recomputed making sure to account new boundary vertices. 

The boundary set defines the list of vertices visited during refinement. So, to further increase efficiency METIS filters this set by more than just if a vertex is on the boundary or not. As the next section will show, a move is accepted only if it leads to positive or zero gain. Therefore, METIS chooses to ignore boundary vertices with negative gain. 

In order to express gain for edge cut we need to introduce two more definitions. Any vertex has an internal and an external degree, which define the sum of the edge weight the vertex has toward different partitions. The internal degree is the sum of the weights of edges from a vertex $v$ to other vertices in the same partition as $v$. We mark the internal degree of a vertex $v$ with $ID[v]$. The external degree is the sum of the weights of edges from $v$ to vertices in partitions other than its own. We mark the external degree of $v$ as $ED[v]$. To simplify calculations during refinement we also specify the external degree towards a specific partition $a$ as $ED_a[v]$.

A vertex $v$ is then added to the boundary set if:
\[
	ED[v] - ID[v] \geq 0.
\]
The specific external degree of vertex will always be smaller or equal to the total external degree. Using Equation~\ref{gain_specific_edgecut} we see that the gain will be positive only if the specific external degree is greater than the internal degree. Consequently, if the total external degree is already less than the internal degree no move will possibly lead to positive gain. 

To optimize refinement even more, the boundary set is ordered by the potential gain a vertex has. Since the actual gain needs to calculated for every possible move a vertex has, METIS makes an estimate for this potential gain. For a vertex $v$ this potential gain is expressed as:
\[
	\frac{ED[v]}{\sqrt{|N_v|}} - ID[v]
\]
where $|N_v|$ is the number of unique partitions $v$ is connected to. This gives a normalized result of what is the probable gain. Using this ordering increases the chance of performing moves with high gain early. 

\subsubsection{Greedy Refinement}
Karypis and Kumar propose an improved version of the k-way refinement KL algorithm in \cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998} referred to as Greedy Refinement (GR). This algorithm achieves high quality partitions while maintaining a computational complexity that is independent of the number of partitions $k$. Traditional KL-based refinement algorithms implement a lookahead strategy, which permits the movement of clusters of vertices across partition boundaries \cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. However, within the context of a multilevel partitioning framework, the benefit of this mechanism diminishes. Since each vertex in a coarser graph $G_i$ represents a cluster of vertices from a finer graph $G_{i-1}$, moving a single vertex in $G_i$ already corresponds to relocating a group of vertices in the original graph. Consequently, the lookahead mechanism becomes redundant. GR eliminates the lookahead step, thereby simplifying the refinement process and infrastructure presented in \cite{hendrickson_improved_1995}. This simplification contributes to both the reduced computational complexity and the practical efficiency of the GR algorithm.

The GR algorithm proceeds through multiple iterations, where each iteration involves visiting all boundary vertices. For each vertex, the algorithm determines whether relocating it to a neighboring partition would improve the total edge cut. GR might in some cases also choose moves that do not change the edge cut, but that lead to an improved balance in the graph. Any move must satisfy the balancing conditions~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. The updated partitioning serves as the input for the next iteration.

For a graph $G_i = (V_i, E_i)$ and its partitioning vector $P_i$, each vertex $v \in V_i$ has a set of neighboring partitions $N_v$. That is, the union of the partitions that the vertices adjacent to $v$ belong to. We denote the set of adjacent vertices to $v$ as $V^{\mathrm{adj}}_v$, we can now define: $N_v = \bigcup_{u \in V^{\mathrm{adj}}_v}P_i[u]$~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. If all neighbors of $v$ are in the same partition as $v$, then $v$ is considered an \textit{interior vertex}, and $N_v = \emptyset$. Conversely, if $v$ has neighbors in different partitions, then $N_v \neq \emptyset$, and the cardinality of $N_v$ can be as large as $V^{\mathrm{adj}}_v$ (in the case where each neighbor belongs to a distinct partition). During refinement, a vertex $v$ can move to any of the partitions present in $N_v$.

For all boundary vertices we consider how a move to a neighbor partition ($N_v$) affects the total edge cut of the graph. 
%In order to do that we need to introduce two more definitions. Any vertex has an internal and an external degree, which define the sum of the edge weight the vertex has toward different partitions. The internal degree is the sum of the weights of edges from a vertex $v$ to other vertices in the same partition as $v$. We mark the internal degree of a vertex $v$ with $ID[v]$. The external degree is the sum of the weights of edges from $v$ to vertices in partitions other than its own. We mark the external degree of $v$ as $ED[v]$. To simplify calculations we specify the external degree towards a specific partition $a$ as $ED_a[v]$. 
For a boundary vertex $v$, moving to a neighbor partition $a \in N_v$ the gain is defined as:
\begin{equation} \label{gain_specific_edgecut}
	\mathrm{gain} = ED_a[v] - ID[v].
\end{equation}
GR will iterate over all partitions in $N_v$ looking for any move that yields a positive gain. The move yielding the highest gain is then chosen as destination. 

To maintain balance, each move needs to either follow the balance constraints of the graph or to improve the balance of the graph. The weight boundaries are defined as follows:
\[
	W^{min} = \frac{W}{k(1 + \epsilon)}
\] 
\[
	W^{max} = \frac{W}{k} \cdot (1 + \epsilon)
\]
meaning that any partition $V_i$, must be $W^{min} < w(V_i) < W^{max}$ also after a move~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. A small side note on the notation for $W^{min}$, which is different than the one found in~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}, is that we based ours on the source code of the partitioner. Their only difference lies in the new expression using the balance constraint $\epsilon$ instead of a constant like in ~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. Additionally, GR accepts moves that improve the load balance in the graph. A move improves balance if the destination partition weighs less than the source after the move~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. This ensures that the destination will not be overloaded by the move, or at least that it would be just as overloaded as the source. For a vertex $v$ we express it as:
\[
	\frac{w(\mathrm{destination})}{k} < \frac{w(\mathrm{source}) - w(v)}{k}.
\]

After each accepted move the boundary set, neighbor partition sets, and the degrees of the affected vertices are updated. GR will go through the boundary set until all vertices in it have been visited. Note that whenever a move is made the set is affected and vertices may get added or deleted from it. Additionally it's also worth mentioning that moved vertices are not allowed to be moved again during the same refinement stage~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. Once a full pass is completed the boundary set is computed again and the process repeats itself. By default in our partitioner this process will repeat itself 10 times with the exception of an early termination if the pass led to no improvement in edge cut or no vertices were moved. Based on their empirical results, Karypis and Kumar observed that GR typically converges within four to eight iterations~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. This relatively fast convergence contributes to the overall efficiency of the multilevel partitioning approach.
