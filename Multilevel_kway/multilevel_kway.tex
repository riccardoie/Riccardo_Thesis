\chapter{Multilevel k-way Graph Partitioning}\label{chap:multilevel-kway}
This chapter presents the Multilevel $k$-way Graph Partitioning (GP) algorithm, one of the most widely used GP algorithms in practice. The multilevel approach scales to large graphs by first coarsening the input down to a small graph, partitioning that, and then projecting the partition back to the original size while refining it at each intermediate level. We cover each of these three phases: coarsening, initial partitioning, and uncoarsening with refinement. 

The chapter is written with a particular focus on the techniques used by METIS (see Section~\ref{sec:Metis very general description}), the partitioner employed in this thesis. The refinement step is treated in the most detail, as it is the phase in which our objective function, NVOL, is integrated. However, NVOL itself is not described here; the next two chapters cover its design and how it plugs into the refinement framework presented in this chapter. The aim of this chapter is to give the reader an understanding of how the multilevel $k$-way graph partitioning algorithm works, before introducing our objective function, NVOL. 

\section{Preliminaries}
The overall structure of a multilevel $k$-way graph partitioning algorithm is fairly straightforward. The process begins by coarsening the input graph until it is reduced to a sufficiently small size. At this coarsest level, an initial $k$-way partitioning is computed using
a graph growing algorithm (see Section~\ref{graph_growing})~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. The graph is then uncoarsened back to its original size in successive steps. At each level of uncoarsening, the partitioning is refined to improve quality by exploiting the increased degrees of freedom available at the finer level. During this phase, the partitions are improved using an objective function (see Section~\ref{subs:objective_functions}). NVOL, our objective function, is implemented in this phase, which is discussed in Chapter~\ref{chapter5}. Karypis and Kumar demonstrate in~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998} that this multilevel approach yields high-quality partitions, often comparable to or better than those produced by recursive bisection~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}.

In Figure~\ref{fig:multilevel-overview}, this process is illustrated, showing how an initial graph $G_0$ is coarsened until it reaches $G_4$, which is smaller in size. At this level, an initial partition is performed on the coarsest graph. After that, the graph is step by step projected back to its original size, refining the partition at each level in order to minimize the chosen objective function~\cite{KarypisGeorge1999PMkP}.

The multilevel partitioning problem uses the same definitions as in Section~\ref{sec:gpp}. A $k$-way partition is represented through a vector $P$. The vector has a length of $n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices in the graph. Each element of $P$ is an integer between 1 and $k$, indicating the partition the vertex belongs to. Specifically, a vertex $v \in V$ assigned to partition $a$ will have $P[v] = a$. The next sections will now explain how each phase works and how the partitions are formed.

\section{Coarsening Phase} \label{sec:coarsening}
During the coarsening phase, a sequence of increasingly smaller graphs is constructed, maintaining a similar structure to the original graph with fewer degrees of freedom. The most common approach is to aggregate groups of vertices into a single coarse vertex through matching~\cite{buluc_recent_2016, KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. Formally, the idea is to contract pairs of matched vertices, where we define a matching as a set of edges no two of which share a vertex~\cite{buluc_recent_2016, KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. Since the goal of coarsening is to reduce the graph size, a matching is considered good if it is composed of many matched edges. To achieve this, \textit{maximal matchings} are used. A matching is maximal if any edge in the graph that is not contained in the matching has at least one of its endpoints matched~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. The following subsection describes in detail the implementation of Sorted Heavy Edge Matching (SHEM), which is the matching algorithm employed by the partitioner we used in this thesis. For further reading on other coarsening algorithms, we refer the reader to~\cite{buluc_recent_2016, KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}. 

Once a matching is performed the coarse vertex has a weight equal to the sum of the weights of all the vertices it contains. Additionally, the coarse vertex receives all the edges of the vertices it contains. Vertices that are not matched are simply copied over to the smaller graph. This process is repeated until we reach a graph $G_m$ with a small enough number of vertices~\cite{karypis_analysis_1995}.

For a given graph $G_i = (V_i, E_i)$, a subset of vertices $V^v_i \subseteq V_i$ is merged into a coarse vertex $v \in V_{i+1}$ for the next coarser graph $G_{i+1}$. The coarse vertex $v$ has weight $w(v) = \sum_{u \in V^v_i} w(u)$. This ensures that the total vertex weight is preserved across coarsening levels. To preserve connectivity, the new vertex $v$ receives the union of the edges of the vertices in $V^v_i$. For any neighboring vertex $u$, the edge weight is $w(\{v,u\}) = \sum_{z\in V^v_i}w(\{z,u\})$. This aggregation strategy preserves structural and weight-related properties of the original graph, thereby enabling efficient and balanced processing at coarser levels~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}.

\subsection{Sorted Heavy Edge Matching}
SHEM begins by generating a random permutation of the graph's vertices, which is then sorted based on vertex degrees. Each vertex gets assigned a bucket based on the square of its degree, sorted from lowest to highest. This ordering ensures that low-degree vertices, which have fewer opportunities for matching, are prioritized and thus more likely to be successfully matched. Delaying them would increase the chance of remaining unmatched. Once the vertices are sorted, they are traversed in order. For each unmatched vertex $u$, the algorithm selects the unmatched neighboring vertex $v$ connected to $u$ via the heaviest incident edge. The edge $\{u,v\}$ is then added to the maximal matching $M_i$, and both $u$ and $v$ are marked as matched. If no suitable neighbor exists, the vertex $u$ remains unmatched. This process continues until all vertices have been processed.

The greedy nature of SHEM tends to preserve strong edge connections in the coarser graph, contributing to a significant reduction in total edge weight and, consequently, in the number of edge-cuts~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH, karypis_parallel_1999}. Karypis and Kumar show in~\cite{karypis_analysis_1995} that the coarser graph has lower total edge weight, and consequently a lower edge-cut. 

\section{Initial Partitioning Phase} \label{sec:initial_partitioning}
Once the graph has been coarsened to the smallest level, the initial partitioning is performed. The goal of this stage is to produce $k$ roughly equal partitions before further refinement. Several partitioning algorithms can be used here, including spectral partitioning~\cite{hendrickson_multilevel_1995}, graph growing partitioning~\cite{KarypisGeorge1998AFaH}, and multilevel recursive bisection~\cite{simon_recursive_bisection}, among others. Since the graph is relatively small at this stage, the partitioning is fast. The partitioner we used, METIS, uses a multilevel recursive bisection approach.
%Using the definition in Section~\ref{sec:gpp} we use graph growing partitioning (GGP) as the default bisection algorithm.

The multilevel recursive bisection approach takes the coarse graph and bisects it using graph growing partitioning (GGP, see Section~\ref{graph_growing}). The resulting two subgraphs are then each bisected again. This process is repeated recursively until the desired number of partitions is reached. When the number of partitions is not a power of 2, the algorithm keeps bisecting until one subgraph has 3 partitions remaining. At this level only one of the produced subgraphs is bisected further. Consider Figure~\ref{fig:tree}, where graph $G$ is to be partitioned into 7 groups. At the first level, $G_0$ has 4 partitions remaining while $G_1$ has 3. Since $G_1$ has only 3 partitions remaining, only $G_4$ is bisected further. The left side ($G_0$), on the other hand, continues bisecting until all partitions have been produced.

During each bisecting step, GGP is used. As described in Section~\ref{graph_growing}, this bisection approach is based on breadth-first search (BFS). Starting from a random seed it tries to group together vertices connected to each other. At every step it checks that both partitions maintain a weight threshold. Once a partition is computed the graph is further refined using the refinement algorithm proposed by Fiduccia and Mattheyses (FM) in \cite{fiduccia_fm}. The FM refinement algorithm iterates through the boundary vertices of the graph, that is, vertices that share at least one edge with a vertex in the other partition. At each step the vertex move that yields the greatest reduction in edge-cut (see Section~\ref{subs:objective_functions}) is selected. To avoid a subpar solution, FM allows a limited number of moves that worsen the edge-cut. This process is repeated over the boundary vertices several times, keeping the partition with the lowest edge-cut.

Despite the non-deterministic nature of the GGP algorithm, METIS produces the same result for identical re-runs. METIS uses a fixed default seed, so the ``random'' choice is the same across re-runs. In other words, if the seed is not manually passed, the initial partition will always be the same when partitioning a graph with the same parameters multiple times. 

It is worth noting that for our volume based objective function we also use this approach based on edge-cut for the initial partition. There are a few reasons for this. Firstly, the initial partition is going to be heavily refined later during the uncoarsening phase, making the quality of the initial partition not as important. Additionally, algorithms like FM are fast and work well with edge-cut; using another objective function would make this phase significantly slower. For these reasons, it is reasonable to use edge-cut as the objective function for this first partitioning. 

\section{Uncoarsening Phase} \label{sec:uncoarsening}
The final step is the uncoarsening and refinement phase, during which the graph is gradually projected back to its original size. The coarsest graph $G_m$ is successively expanded through finer representations $G_{m-1}, G_{m-2}, \dots, G_1$. At each projection from $G_{i+1}$ to $G_i$, every vertex contained within a coarse vertex $v$ inherits the partition assignment that $v$ held in $G_{i+1}$. Once all coarse vertices at a given level have been expanded, the resulting partition is improved using local refinement heuristics.

The partitioner we used, METIS, employs a variant of the Kernighan-Lin (KL) algorithm \cite{Kernighan_kl}, called Greedy Refinement (GR), to refine the graph. The KL algorithm iteratively swaps vertices among partitions of a bisection to minimize an objective function, continuing until no further improvement is possible and a local minimum is reached~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. Refining a $k$-way partition is significantly more complex since each vertex has $k - 1$ possible moves as opposed to just one move in a bisection~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. This substantially enlarges the optimization space and increases the computational cost, since a gain must be calculated for each possible move. The gain represents the improvement in the objective function's value resulting from relocating a vertex to a new partition.

The refinement procedure described above is general; what changes between objective functions is how the gain of a move is computed. In the rest of this section, we present the standard edge-cut-based formulation used by METIS, as it is the simplest case and makes the underlying mechanics easiest to follow. The total communication volume objective function and our partition-aware variant, NVOL, both build on this same refinement framework and use the GR algorithm. They differ in how the gain is computed and which moves are accepted; these are covered in Chapter~\ref{cv_calculation} and Chapter~\ref{chapter5}, respectively.

\subsection{Projection and preparation before refinement}\label{sec:preparation for refinement}
Once the graph is projected up a level, it undergoes a preparation phase before actual refinement. The new finer graph contains new vertices and edges, requiring a full recomputation of the underlying structures describing the graph. The whole graph is therefore traversed in order to obtain accurate information about the new connections in this finer level.

During this phase, the boundary set, the set that defines the vertices visited during refinement, is recomputed. By definition, a vertex is on the boundary of a partition if it has at least one edge to another partition. However, to further increase efficiency, METIS filters this set using more than just whether a vertex is on the boundary. As the next section will show, a move is accepted only if it leads to positive or zero gain. Therefore, METIS chooses to ignore boundary vertices with negative gain.

In order to express gain for edge-cut, we need three definitions: internal degree, external degree, and the external degree towards a specific partition. Every vertex has a degree, which represents the number of edges incident to it. This degree can be divided into internal and external degree, which define the number of edges a vertex has toward different partitions. The internal degree is the number of edges from a vertex $v$ to other vertices in the same partition as $v$. We denote the internal degree of a vertex $v$ by $ID[v]$. The external degree is the number of edges from $v$ to vertices in partitions other than its own. We denote the external degree of $v$ by $ED[v]$. To simplify calculations during refinement, we also specify the external degree toward a specific partition $a$ as $ED_a[v]$.

A vertex $v$ is then added to the boundary set if:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:boundary_filter}
	ED[v] - ID[v] \geq 0.
\end{equation}
This expression tells us whether a vertex has the potential to be moved or not. For the edge-cut objective function, gain is computed as the specific external degree minus the internal degree (see Equation~\ref{gain_specific_edgecut}). The specific external degree will always be smaller than or equal to the total external degree, which means that if the expression above is negative, the gain will also be negative, and no move will be performed. Therefore, to improve efficiency, METIS only adds vertices to the boundary set if they satisfy Equation~\ref{eq:boundary_filter}.

To optimize refinement even further, the boundary set is ordered by the potential gain of each vertex. Since the actual gain needs to be calculated for every possible destination a vertex has, METIS estimates the potential gain. For a vertex $v$, the potential gain is expressed as:
\[
\frac{ED[v]}{\sqrt{|N_v|}} - ID[v]
\]
where $|N_v|$ is the number of unique partitions (apart from its own) that $v$ is connected to. This gives a normalized estimate of the probable gain. Using this ordering increases the chance of performing high-gain moves early. This set is then used by the refinement algorithm to refine the partitioning quality. 

\subsection{Greedy Refinement} \label{sec:greedy_refinement}
In~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}, Karypis and Kumar propose an improved version of the $k$-way KL refinement algorithm, referred to as Greedy Refinement (GR). This algorithm achieves high-quality partitions while maintaining a computational complexity that is independent of the number of partitions $k$. Traditional KL-based refinement algorithms implement a lookahead strategy, which permits the movement of clusters of vertices across partition boundaries~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. However, within the context of a multilevel partitioning framework, the benefit of this mechanism diminishes. Since each vertex in a coarser graph $G_i$ represents a cluster of vertices in a finer graph $G_{i-1}$, moving a single vertex in $G_i$ already corresponds to relocating a group of vertices. Consequently, the lookahead mechanism becomes redundant. GR eliminates the lookahead step, thereby simplifying the refinement process and infrastructure presented in~\cite{hendrickson_improved_1995}. This simplification contributes to both the reduced computational complexity and the practical efficiency of the GR algorithm. GR is independent of the choice of objective function. In this section, we describe the main components of the algorithm and use edge-cut as a concrete example to illustrate how gain drives the choice of a move. The next two chapters will show how NVOL performs these calculations. 

The GR algorithm proceeds through multiple iterations, in which each iteration visits all boundary vertices. For each vertex, the algorithm determines whether relocating it to a neighboring partition would improve the objective function's value. In some cases, GR may also choose moves that do not change this value but instead lead to improved balance in the graph. Any move must satisfy the balancing conditions~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. The updated partitioning serves as the input for the next iteration.

For a graph $G_i = (V_i, E_i)$ and its partitioning vector $P_i$, each vertex $v \in V_i$ has a set of neighboring partitions $N_v$, defined as the union of the partitions (apart from $v$'s own) which its adjacent vertices belong to. We denote the set of vertices adjacent to $v$ by $V^{\mathrm{adj}}_v$. If all neighbors of $v$ are in the same partition as $v$, then $v$ is considered an \textit{interior vertex}, and $N_v = \emptyset$. Conversely, if $v$ has neighbors in different partitions, then $N_v \neq \emptyset$, and the cardinality of $N_v$ can be as large as $|V^{\mathrm{adj}}_v|$ (when each neighbor belongs to a distinct partition). During refinement, a vertex $v$ can move to any of the partitions in $N_v$.
 
For all boundary vertices, we consider how a move to a neighboring partition (a partition in $N_v$) affects the objective function's value. For edge-cut, the gain of moving a boundary vertex $v$ to a neighboring partition $a \in N_v$ is defined as:
\begin{equation} \label{gain_specific_edgecut}
	\mathrm{gain} = ED_a[v] - ID[v].
\end{equation}
GR iterates over all partitions in $N_v$, looking for any move that yields a positive gain. The move yielding the highest gain is then chosen as the destination.
To maintain balance, each move must either satisfy the graph's balance constraints or improve its balance. The weight boundaries are defined as follows:
\[
W^{min} = \frac{W}{k(1 + \epsilon)}
\] 
\[
W^{max} = \frac{W}{k} \cdot (1 + \epsilon)
\]
meaning that any partition $a$ must satisfy $W^{min} < w(a) < W^{max}$, even after a move~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. Note that our notation for $W^{min}$ differs from the one in~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}; we based ours on the partitioner's source code. The only difference is that our expression uses the balance constraint $\epsilon$, whereas~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998} uses a constant. Additionally, as a tiebreaker for moves that yield the same gain, GR accepts moves that improve the load balance in the graph. A move improves balance if its destination is a lighter partition, promoting a more equal distribution of vertex weights across partitions~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}.
%This ensures that a destination will not be overloaded by the move, or at least that it would be the best option to avoid that. 
For a vertex $v$, this condition is expressed as:
\[
	w(\mathrm{destination}) < w(\mathrm{source}) - w(v).
\]

After each accepted move, the boundary set, neighbor partition sets, and the degrees of the affected vertices are updated. GR continues through the boundary set until all vertices have been visited. Note that whenever a move is made, the boundary set is affected, and vertices may be added to or removed from it. It is also worth mentioning that moved vertices cannot be moved again during the same refinement stage~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. Once a full pass is completed, the boundary set is computed again and the process repeats. By default, METIS repeats this process up to 10 times, terminating early if a pass yields no improvement in the objective function's value or moves no vertices. Based on their empirical results, Karypis and Kumar observe that GR typically converges within four to eight iterations~\cite{karypis_multilevelk-way_1998}. This relatively fast convergence contributes to the overall efficiency of the multilevel partitioning approach.
